BritishNationalGrid.uk – Coordinate Conversion and Tile Reference Tool

A long-running web app designed to support the British geospatial community with a lightweight tool for coordinate conversion and spatial referencing. It offers a clean, browser-based way to explore the British National Grid, convert between coordinate formats, and better understand the informal quadrant-based tile naming convention often used in large raster datasets.

Why I Built It

Running my geospatial software consultancy business, 3XE Ltd, gave me a professional interest in improving clarity around the way spatial data is subdivided and referenced in the UK. While standard OS grid references and coordinate transformations are well-served by existing tools, the ‘cardinal quadrant’ notation — such as SS10NW or SU97SE — is often encountered in academic and professional datasets without much explanation.

BritishNationalGrid.uk helps users visualise and work with that system more intuitively, whether they’re identifying files for aerial imagery, working with map tiles, or teaching the structure of the grid.

As a concrete example: an aerial survey dataset might contain a file named SU97NE.tiff, referring to the northeastern quadrant of the 10km square SU97 — which includes Windsor Castle. But the 5km square directly to the east of SU97NE won't appear adjacent in a list of files in the dataset. Rather, it belongs to a different 10km and 100km tile and is named TQ07NW.tiff. This is the kind of nuance that BritishNationalGrid.uk is designed to clarify at a glance.

Features

  • Convert between easting/northing, OS grid references, and WGS84 lat/lon
  • Interactive overlay of the national grid on Ordnance Survey mapping
  • Visualisation and handling of 5km quadrant suffixes (e.g. NW, SE, etc.)
  • Keyboard shortcuts and copy-friendly formatting for ease of use
  • Optimised for fast loading and responsive use across devices

Longevity and Adoption

The app has grown steadily since launch, with no marketing beyond word of mouth. It now serves around 600 visits per day, with a portion of those being daily returning users — many of whom appear to rely on it as part of their regular workflows.

One recent piece of feedback simply read: “Excellent!” — and in many ways, that quietly appreciative tone reflects the project’s goal.

What It Represents

This is one of several small tools I’ve built to make spatial information more accessible. It reflects a broader interest in simplifying geospatial workflows and building interfaces that feel purposeful rather than overwhelming — particularly in contexts where clarity matters more than feature depth.
